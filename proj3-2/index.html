Part 2: Microfacet Material

Building off of Part 3-1, in this part we implement the BSDF for the microfacet model based on rough, reflective surfaces. The overall BSDF function is given below and sampled in our ray tracer:

There are three main parts to this equation. The first, F(wi)F(w_i)F(wi​)﻿, is the Fresnel term, which is based on the specific properties of our rough, conductive surfaces.

We compute θi\theta_iθi​﻿ as the angle between wiw_iwi​﻿ and the normal vector, and then the constants η\etaη﻿ and kkk﻿ are indices of refraction for conductors. For our initial demos, we use the indices for gold. Furthermore, we would ideally compute these indices at every wavelength of light, but for simplicity, η\etaη﻿ and kkk﻿ are 3-dimensional vectors for refractivity indices at red (614 nm), green (549 nm), and blue (466 nm).

The next term, D(h)D(h)D(h)﻿ is a normal distribution function to describe how the microfacets’ normals are distributed, that is formulated from the Beckmann distribution.

This term varies by the value α, which corresponds to the roughness of the material. The rougher the material, the less shiny it becomes, as the surface will become more diffuse. A smaller alpha will generate a more glossy and specular material, meaning that there are more reflections of light on that material.

Lastly, G(wo,wi)G(w_o, w_i)G(wo​,wi​)﻿ is the shadow masking term, which we did not need to implement ourselves.

Show two images of scene CBbunny_microfacet_cu.dae rendered using cosine hemisphere sampling (default) and your importance sampling. The sampling rate should be fixed at 64 samples per pixel and 1 samples per light. The number of bounces should be at least 5. Briefly discuss their difference.

In the images below, the cosine hemisphere sampling method on the left is a lot noisier than the importance sampling method on the right. While cosine hemisphere sampling is good for diffuse BRDFs, in our case, it doesn’t match well with the Beckmann distribution we implemented for computing the D(h)D(h)D(h)﻿ term. As a result, the cosine hemisphere sampling does not converge with our given samples in many parts of the rendered image.

Instead, we importance sample the BRDF FFF﻿ in accordance with the Beckmann distribution, which results in a less noisy, better-converging rendering on the right.

Part 4: Depth of Field

In a few sentences, explain the differences between a pinhole camera model and a thin-lens camera model.

In a pinhole camera model, everything is in focus, whereas in the thin-lens camera model, the focus is based on the camera’s focalDistance. This is because we assume the thin-lens camera model refracts light like a lens. In a pinhole camera model, the light goes straight through the pinhole point as shown by the red ray in the diagram below, so light reflecting off of any point in a scene can only enter the camera through a single direction. The radiance is only sampled from the center of the lens.

In a thin-lens camera, we allow sampling through multiple points on the lens, based on our set lens radius. With a non-zero lens radius, only objects in our scene at a certain distance appear in focus since their right rays still refract to the same point, whereas the foreground and background appear blurry since their light rays do not converge. The point of focus can be changed via the focalDistance.

Show a "focus stack" where you focus at 4 visibly different depths through a scene. Make sure to include all screenshots.

In the sequence of images below, we adjusted the -d parameter, which changes the depths through a scene, otherwise known as the focalDistance. The focalDistance determines which parts of the scene are in focus. In the first image, the very back corner of the Cornell Box is in focus, and the rest of the dragon and Cornell Box (which is closer to the camera than the back corner) is out of focus. As the focalDistance (depth d value) decreases, the point of focus on the scene shifts more to the foreground (closer to the camera); first, the dragon’s tail is in focus, then the center of its body, until the focalDistance is close enough where the mouth of the dragon is the point in focus for the scene.
