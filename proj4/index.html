Overview

In this project, we simulated a cloth that was based on a spring and mass physical system. In Part 1, we created the spring-mass mesh, which consisted of structural constraints, shearing constraints, and bending constraints (springs). In Part 2, we added the physical properties to the mesh by integrating physical motion and force equations to calculate where the mass positions would be in each time step. This simulated forces such as gravity on the cloth and the forces from the springs in the mesh.

In Part 3, we handled what would happen if the cloth collided with other objects such as falling onto spheres and planes. We did this by checking to see if points on the cloth had “passed through” the object it was going to collide with, and moving it back to right on the object’s surface, modifying the vector normals so the physical integration from Part 2 still applied and the cloth moved correctly. In Part 4, we handled what would happen when the cloth collided with itself, such as folding onto itself. We used a hash table to separate the masses into a spatial map and made sure that masses that appeared in the same small area did not occupy the same position. This fixed the issue where the cloth would fold through itself.

Finally, in Part 5, we added different shader programs to enable different textures for our cloth, including diffuse shading, Blinn-Phong shading, texture mapping (applying an image to the objects), bump mapping, displacement mapping, and environment-mapped reflections (mirroring).

We learned a lot in this project about shaders and modeling objects where physics is involved. It was really rewarding to see our cloth move in response to different forces and behave realistically, and to use the GUI to modify different components and see how the cloth’s physics changed.

Describe the effects of changing the spring constant ks; how does the cloth behave from start to rest with a very low ks? A high ks?

With a low spring constant ksk_sks​﻿, in the final resting state the cloth droops a lot more. You can see in the screenshots below that as ksk_sks​﻿ increases, the “droopiness” of the cloth decreases and the form of the cloth is more held together. The reason for this observed behavior is because the calculation for external forces on each point mass from a spring is shown in the equation below. The greater the spring constant ksk_sks​﻿, the more force the spring is applying on the point mass, keeping the cloth from becoming “loose”. This is because for a higher ksk_sks​﻿, the spring is more resistant to being lengthened by gravity whereas at a lower ksk_sks​﻿ gravity is more dominant, especially on the top crease.

What about for density?

When we decrease the density, meaning each point mass has a smaller mass, the cloth seems to hang a bit higher at the top, so there isn’t as much of a visible crease. This is because gravity pulls less strongly on the less dense cloth so the top stays more in line with the pinned masses. Likewise, when we increase the density, the crease at the top of the cloth hangs lower at the resting state. This is based on the basic force equation, F=maF = maF=ma﻿.

What about for damping?

The damping term affects how long the cloth takes to converge to its final resting state as well as how fast the point mass positions shift in each time step. This is because damping simulates loss of energy from to friction. For a damping of 0%, the cloth did not converge in any reasonable amount of time and swung around a lot, whereas with damping of 1%, the cloth did not wiggle at all and fell straight to its final state. While the cloth is falling, a lower damping rate also allows the cloth to “flutter” around a bit more while a higher damping rate makes the cloth fall down in a very stiff manner, and more slowly.

Note that the damping of 0% was still in motion when screenshotted since it had not converged to its resting state quickly.

Vary the density as well as ks and describe with words and screenshots how they affect the behavior of the cloth as it falls on itself.

When the density varies, the size and amount of folds and creases change as the cloth falls down. When the density is low, the cloth is lighter and so the cloth can float a bit more and there are not as many small folds on the cloth from the force of gravity pulling it down. When density is higher, the force of gravity has a larger effect, and the masses in the cloth fall such that there are more small folds when the cloth falls. We can see in the final state once the cloth has fully fallen that the higher density cloth has more areas of the cloth flush with the ground, whereas the lower density cloth is not as tight to the ground.

Decreasing the ksk_sks​﻿ term makes the force from the springs weaker and thus the cloth is less likely to keep its original structure. As a result, as the cloth collides with itself upon hitting the plane, the force due to gravity pulls more of the cloth straight down so the cloth folds and creases faster onto itself in a narrower space. On the other hand, increasing the ksk_sks​﻿ term makes the spring forces stronger so the cloth better keeps its rigidity. As a result, as the cloth falls upon itself, it is more resistant to folding so it folds in bigger segments, spreading out more.

Part 5: Shaders

Explain in your own words what is a shader program and how vertex and fragment shaders work together to create lighting and material effects.

Shader programs are helpful for speeding up rendering runtimes because they are separate programs that can run in parallel with the rendering. This is better than using raytracing which can be slow. They take in an input and output a 4-dimensional vector. Shader programs link together vertex and fragment shaders, which can help create cool texture effects. A vertex shader transforms the vertex positions of the object based on forces and outputs a final position. The fragment shader takes in the output of the vertex shader and calculates a color based on the vertices and texture mapping we want to use. By being able to customize vertex position and light effects (color), a lot of materials can be recreated using shader programs.

Explain the Blinn-Phong shading model in your own words. Show a screenshot of your Blinn-Phong shader outputting only the ambient component, a screen shot only outputting the diffuse component, a screen shot only outputting the specular component, and one using the entire Blinn-Phong model.

The Blinn-Phong shading model combines different lighting components (ambient, diffuse, and specular) to output a lighting calculation that has shading and reflection. The ambient component does not change based on lighting direction/source and is a uniform contributor. The reflection and bright highlights come from the specular component, and the diffuse component outputs a matte material effect.

As shown in the images below using the texture texture_3.png, bump mapping gives the appearance of having a 3D surface, but that is only due to color/lighting, and the surface itself is still smooth and flat (unchanged). For bump mapping (and all shaders we’ve worked on so far, we have only been modifying the .frag fragment shader files, which only contribute to the lighting of the fragments. By using lighting, the shader can create a texture to look 3D after grabbing the colors from the texture images.

On the other hand in displacement mapping, the surface is actually getting displaced and thus we can see a 3D surface and no longer a smooth surface. This is because the displacement shader will use both color (.frag fragment shader files) and position changes (.vert vertex shader files). In the .vert vertex shader file, transformations are applied to the vertices, modifying the vertex positions and thus creating the 3D displacement effect. Coupled with the same lighting (color) effects as in bump mapping, it produces a rendering that looks 3D, visually and physically.

Compare how the two shaders react to the sphere by changing the sphere mesh's coarseness by using -o 16 -a 16 and then -o 128 -a 128.

When the mesh coarseness is increased, the bump mapping rendering doesn’t really change because the physical location of the vertices doesn’t change. By increasing the coarseness, in displacement mapping there is a change in the roughness of the surface - when looking at the sides of the sphere (especially the top), the surface becomes rougher and has smaller bumps, and is more granular. The less coarse render has higher and smoother peaks of surface displacement. This is likely because the higher resolution (coarser mesh) causes the displacements to be less steep/extreme and instead there are a larger number of smaller bumps.
